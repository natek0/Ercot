1. The one-sentence problem

The plan's headline pitch is "RTC+B forced batteries to hold enough charge to back their reserve sales, and my duration-value curve prices that coupling — so Q2 (the shadow price) and Q3 (the duration curve) are the same computation." That pitch has a hole: the reserve products that actually create the binding coupling point in the opposite direction from the one the duration curve can see.

2. The mechanism, in plain terms — up vs down, floor vs ceiling

A reserve is a promise to change output on command. There are two directions:

  • Upward reserve = "be ready to push power out (inject) fast." That needs charge in the tank, so it imposes a floor: keep at least so much stored. ERCOT's upward products: Reg-Up, RRS, ECRS, Non-Spin.
  • Downward reserve = "be ready to soak power up (charge) fast." That needs empty space, so it imposes a ceiling: keep at least so much room free. ERCOT's only downward product: Reg-Down.

These are the plan's two energy-headroom constraints (section III.17). \(S^+_t\) is the charge you'll be holding after you act; \(\tau_k\) is product \(k\)'s duration requirement; \(u^k_t\) is how much of it you sold:

\[ \text{(EH-up)}\qquad \underbrace{S^+_t - S^{\min}}_{\text{charge above the floor}} \;\ge\; \frac{1}{\eta_d}\sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}^{\text{up}}} \tau_k\, u^k_t \qquad\text{shadow price } \psi^{\text{up}}_t \] \[ \text{(EH-dn)}\qquad \underbrace{E^{\max} - S^+_t}_{\text{room below the ceiling}} \;\ge\; \eta_c\sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}^{\text{dn}}} \tau_k\, u^k_t \qquad\text{shadow price } \psi^{\text{dn}}_t \]

A shadow price is just "how many extra dollars I'd make if this rule were loosened by one unit." \(\psi^{\text{up}}\) measures the cost of the hold-charge floor; \(\psi^{\text{dn}}\) the cost of the keep-room ceiling.

Duration is a ceiling, so the duration curve can only see the ceiling side

"Duration" is tank size \(E^{\max}\). The plan's headline identity (section IV.11) is a mechanical consequence of the envelope theorem — the value of extra capacity equals the shadow prices of the constraints in which \(E^{\max}\) literally appears:

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{V}^\star}{\partial E^{\max}} \;=\; \sum_t \Big(\underbrace{\bar\lambda_t}_{\text{arbitrage room}} \;+\; \underbrace{\psi^{\text{dn}}_t}_{\text{down-reserve room}}\Big) \]

\(E^{\max}\) appears in the plain capacity limit \(S_t \le E^{\max}\) (shadow price \(\bar\lambda_t\)) and in (EH-dn). It does not appear in (EH-up). So \(\psi^{\text{up}}\) is absent from the duration curve — and that's correct, not a bug: a bigger tank raises the ceiling, and a higher ceiling cannot relieve a floor. This is the plan's own equation; the plan's prose just forgets it in three places (section 5 below).

Why this is the crux

The whole plan is built on the idea that the coupling binds. But "does it bind?" splits into two questions with opposite likely answers:

QuestionWhich products drive itLikely to bind?Feeds the duration curve?
Does the floor bind? \((\psi^{\text{up}}>0)\)Reg-Up, RRS, ECRS, Non-Spin — the big marketsYes, plausibly often. Non-Spin's 4 h requirement genuinely constrains the mostly-short fleet.No — \(\psi^{\text{up}}\) is not in the identity.
Does the ceiling bind? \((\psi^{\text{dn}}>0)\)Reg-Down only — a small, cheap marketRarely. A battery usually has empty room, and Reg-Down volumes/prices are low.Yes — it's the identity's only ancillary term.

So a naive Step 0 that reads the mechanism off the duration curve could report "the ancillary contribution to duration value is ~0 → the mechanism is dead → re-scope," while the economically real coupling (\(\psi^{\text{up}}\), the up-reserve floor) is alive and simply unmeasured. That is the confusion we kept hitting.

The subtlety that makes the plan's decomposition wrong

Up-reserve does affect duration value — but indirectly. To sell 4-hour Non-Spin, a short battery must be pushed toward full (it needs \(\tfrac{1}{\eta_d}\tau_k u^k\) of charge on hand), so its ceiling \(S_t\le E^{\max}\) binds. Extra capacity relieves that — and the benefit shows up in \(\bar\lambda\), the "arbitrage room" multiplier, bundled together with genuine arbitrage value and impossible to separate by looking at the multipliers. Therefore the plan's Q3 deliverable 4 ("decompose the duration curve into an arbitrage part and an ancillary part by splitting the multipliers") is not sound. The fix is a counterfactual decomposition — solve with and without each product set and difference the curves — which attributes value correctly no matter how it routes through the multipliers.

4. Option C, and why it is the right call for the interview goal

Report both objects as distinct, and fix the decomposition. Concretely:

  • Headline A — the cost of SOC enforcement: the distribution of \(\psi^{\text{up}}_t\) on the big upward products (RRS, ECRS, Non-Spin). This is the number every ERCOT operator has been asking since December, it's RTC+B-specific, and it's the one most likely to actually bind.
  • Headline B — the duration-value curve: kept, but presented honestly as driven by arbitrage room \(\bar\lambda\) plus the minor down-reserve market \(\psi^{\text{dn}}\), with the up-reserve effect shown by counterfactual solves rather than a multiplier split.
  • The relationship stated precisely: Q2 and Q3 come from one solve and illuminate the same SOC coupling from its two sides — the floor (what it costs, \(\psi^{\text{up}}\)) and the ceiling (what capacity is worth, \(\bar\lambda+\psi^{\text{dn}}\)). Related, not identical.

Why C beats the alternatives, for your specific goal:

  • vs. A (duration curve stays sole headline, add Reg-Down): the ancillary term stays tiny (Reg-Down is minor) and pulling Regulation into a deterministic LP reintroduces the "Regulation looks free" artifact your Decision 2 exists to avoid, which only the Step-4 stochastic model can fix. Weakest.
  • vs. B (make \(\psi^{\text{up}}\) the only headline): fine and defensible, but throws away the duration curve — which is the legible, visual, "curve not a point" deliverable that makes the project readable to a non-specialist. No reason to discard it.
  • C keeps every deliverable, corrects the one real error, and turns the up/down asymmetry into evidence of judgment. An ERCOT-literate interviewer will probe "your capacity derivative only has the downward multiplier — reconcile that with the coupling binding upward." Pre-empting that is the single highest-leverage thing in the project. C is you pre-empting it.

Bonus: C makes your Decision 2 coherent instead of confusing. Under C, the contingency-only run isn't "the headline duration run" (which never made sense — it zeroes out \(\psi^{\text{dn}}\)); it's "the clean run for the up-reserve SOC cost \(\psi^{\text{up}}\)," which is Headline A. The all-products run is where the small \(\psi^{\text{dn}}\) / duration story lives. The two runs now have clear, separate jobs.
